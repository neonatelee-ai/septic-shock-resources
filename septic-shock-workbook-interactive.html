Recognising Septic Shock in a Child

The Case — Rebecca, Age 4 (20 kg)

A 4-year-old girl presents to the paediatric ED. Her mother says she is "not herself" and seems lethargic. She has had a fever and cough for three days. Today she is less responsive, won't eat or drink, and has had no urine output all day. She was brought straight into the resuscitation bay.

  • CVS: No murmur; capillary refill 5 seconds centrally; eyes sunken
  • Respiratory: Good air entry bilaterally; right-sided rhonchi (infection source)
  • Skin: No rashes, no petechiae, no hot joints
  • GU: Mother reports no urine output today (oliguria)

Recognising Paediatric Septic Shock

Children compensate cardiovascularly for longer than adults — they increase heart rate to maintain blood pressure, so hypotension is a late and ominous sign. Do not wait for hypotension to diagnose shock.

The paediatric sepsis continuum: SIRS → Sepsis → Severe Sepsis → Septic Shock. Shock is present when infection causes cardiovascular dysfunction despite adequate fluid resuscitation.

⚕ Clinical Pearl
In a child with tachycardia, prolonged capillary refill (>2 seconds), and altered level of consciousness — assume septic shock and begin resuscitation immediately. Do not wait for a confirmed source or positive cultures.

Anatomy & Pathophysiology
Why the Body Fails in Septic Shock

The Septic Cascade

Sepsis results from a dysregulated host response to infection. In this case, pneumonia is the source — bacteria release toxins that trigger a systemic inflammatory reaction far beyond what is needed to fight the local infection.

  1. Pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) — e.g. bacterial lipopolysaccharide (LPS) — bind toll-like receptors on macrophages.
  2. Macrophages release a cytokine storm: IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α, triggering widespread endothelial activation.
  3. Endothelial dysfunction causes vasodilation (low SVR → hypotension) and capillary leak (plasma shifts into interstitium → hypovolaemia).
  4. Cytokines also directly depress myocardial contractility, reducing cardiac output.
  5. The net result: oxygen delivery falls → tissues switch to anaerobic metabolism → lactic acidosis → multi-organ dysfunction.
🔬 Mechanism Summary
Septic shock = distributive shock (vasodilation) + hypovolaemic component (capillary leak) + sometimes cardiogenic (myocardial depression). This is why treatment requires both fluids AND vasopressors.

Why Children Are Uniquely Vulnerable

Limited Glycogen Stores

Children have proportionally smaller livers and therefore far less hepatic glycogen than adults. During infection, glucose is consumed rapidly by immune cells and by tissues already switching to anaerobic glycolysis. This explains the critical hypoglycaemia (2.4 mmol/L) — a complication rarely seen in septic adults.

⚠ Danger
Untreated hypoglycaemia in a child will cause seizures within minutes, followed by permanent neurological injury. Check glucose in every critically unwell child — it takes seconds and is easily corrected.

Cardiovascular Compensation

Children have limited stroke volume reserve. They increase cardiac output almost entirely via heart rate. This means:

  • Tachycardia is the first and most sensitive sign of circulatory compromise
  • Children can maintain near-normal BP for a surprisingly long time
  • When tachycardia can no longer compensate → rapid decompensation to cardiovascular arrest

Crisis Resource Management

Team Communication in Paediatric Resus

Drug dosing errors are disproportionately common in paediatric emergencies due to weight-based calculations under pressure. Clear communication is the primary safeguard.

  • Designate a single team leader who gives orders — multiple voices giving orders simultaneously leads to confusion and omissions.
  • Close-loop communication: "I want ceftriaxone, 50 mg/kg, that is one gram, via IO" → nurse responds "Confirming: ceftriaxone one gram IO" → gives → "Ceftriaxone one gram IO given at 14:03."
  • Designate a scribe to record all drugs, doses, and times in real time.
  • Read back all doses before administration — errors are most common at the point of ordering.

Managing a Distraught Parent

A distressed parent in the resuscitation bay is one of the most challenging elements of paediatric emergencies. Poorly managed, it can compromise both the parent's experience and the team's performance.

▶ Best Practice
Designate one specific team member — not the team leader — whose sole role is to stay with and support the parent. This person should not have clinical duties during the resuscitation.

Communication Framework for the Parent

  • Acknowledge emotion first: "I can see how frightened you are. We are all focused on Rebecca right now."
  • Provide brief, honest updates in plain language — silence and uncertainty are more distressing than hard truths.
  • If the parent's presence is impeding care, ask them calmly to step to a nearby area: "It will help us most if you wait just outside — I'll come and find you in two minutes."
  • Do not automatically exclude parents — family-witnessed resuscitation is supported by evidence and is the preference of most families.

IO Access — Overcoming Hesitation

In surveys of junior doctors, reluctance to insert IO in a timely manner is cited as a significant cause of delay. The key reframing:

  • IO is not a last resort — it is the established standard after 2 failed IV attempts or 2 minutes in a critically unwell child.
  • All resuscitation drugs, fluids, and blood products that can be given IV can be given IO.
  • IO pharmacokinetics are equivalent to central venous access.
  • Complications are rare: the most common is extravasation, prevented by correct depth and confirming flow.
